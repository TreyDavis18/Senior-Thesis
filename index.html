Bitcoin Background Information

What is Bitcoin?

Cryptocurrencies are a form of decentralized digital currency, meaning that the currency is not stored by any bank or company. Normal currencies are tracked and regulated by banks who will verify transactions to keep an eye on what is going on with the currencies that they regulate. Cryptocurrencies however, use a process of “trustless verification” to verify transactions, meaning that there is no central agency who has the ledger of transactions. The “ledgers”, also referred to as blockchain, for different cryptocurrencies are kept by everyone who takes part in mining each particular cryptocurrency. (3Blue1Brown, 2017) This website will focus on the incredibly popular cryptocurrency called Bitcoin.

Who created Bitcoin?

Cryptocurrencies first made their debut in 2009 with the now insanely popular Bitcoin. No one knows for sure who created Bitcoin, however there is a name attributed to the creation of it. A (claimed) Japanese man named Satoshi Nakamoto is the creator of Bitcoin, however it is widely assumed that this name is a pseudonym and may even be a group of people.

What is mining for Bitcoin?

Mining for Bitcoin is how new Bitcoins are added into circulation. It involves solving equations on a blockchain that are typically encrypted using SHA-256, a type of hash encryption. These equations are public records of transactions using Bitcoin, and solving these equations proves that the transactions are valid. Each block on the blockchain is a group of transaction records that needs to be verified through mining. When a user becomes the first person to solve a transaction block (mine a block) they are awarded with a certain amount of bitcoin.

How does Bitcoin mining work?

Cryptomining utilizes computers containing multiple high-end and extremely powerful graphics cards that work to solve the encrypted transactions on a blockchain. High end GPUs (Graphics Processing Units) are very good at solving thousands of sophisticated algorithms every second, which is exactly what cryptominers need in order to be the first person to solve a transaction record. Because these computer set-ups typically have multiple GPUs running at max power at the same time, they also need a lot of cooling to stop the cards from overheating.

Bitcoin's Energy Consumption

Yearly Energy Consumption in MWh

As of 2021, Bitcoin has a total annual energy consumption of 91 million MWh, this puts the energy consumption of Bitcoin above 37 of the states in the United States of America. 91 million MWh could power an avereage househould for over 8,272 years, or all of Vermont for 8 years.

How much energy is used to mine a bitcoin?

Mining for Bitcoin is not an energy efficient process. As of August 2021, it uses around the same amount of energy that a typical household would use in 8 years. This is a little bit better than it was, hitting a max energy consumption of the average household over 13 years in early 2021, but this is still not a sustainable amount of energy for Bitcoin. (Huang et al., 2021)

How much energy is used on a Bitcoin transaction?

A report from 2021 stated that every Bitcoin transaction uses over 1,000 kWh of electricity. (Tully, 2021) Even using Bitcoin just to buy a sandwich, uses more energy than an average household uses in six weeks, or enough energy to drive an average electric car for over 3,300 miles (That's enough to drive from coast to coast in the United States). 1,000 kWh is equal to over $100 worth of energy for every single transaction.

What does Bitcoin's energy consumption look like compared to other energy consumptions?

1,000 kWh per Bitcoin transaction is the same as:

    Using an oven stovetop for 666 hours

    Watching shows on a 55” 4K LED TV for 8,333 hours

    Making waffles for 3,030 hours (at 5 minutes per waffle, that is 36,360 waffles)

    Running a pedestal fan for 33,333 hours (3.8 years)

    Making 3,846 single serve coffees

    Playing PS4 for 5.5 years

Environmentally conscious option for Bitcoin

Bitcoin uses code that is called “proof of work”. This necessitates a large amount of computers in order to verify and secure the transactions that take place using Bitcoin. One very possible option for Bitcoin is to follow in the path of another highly popular cryptocurrency called Ethereum. Ethereum uses a code process called “proof of stake”. Proof of stake means that miners use their own coins as a pledge, with this pledge they are given a chance to verify transactions that use Ethereum, becoming a validator. This means that not everyone is allowed to mine for Ethereum, only those who have put in the stake to earn a spot as a validator. A group made up of Greenpeace USA, Environmental Working Group, and other groups fighting against the environmental effects of cryptomining have stated that changing Bitcoin from proof of work to proof of stake could reduce the energy consumption of Bitcoin by nearly 99%. (Rushe, 2022)
